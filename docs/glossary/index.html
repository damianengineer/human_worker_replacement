Glossary

You'll see some of these words a lot on this site — and in news stories about AI and jobs. Here's what they actually mean, in plain language, so you're not stuck guessing.

Each term also lists two sources: Origin is where the term or idea first came from, and Current is a more recent, credible source you can read for more detail.

Core AI Terms

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Computer systems that perform tasks normally requiring human thinking — understanding language, recognizing images, solving problems, and making decisions.

Origin: McCarthy, J., et al., 1955/2006 — source needed

Current: Russell, S., & Norvig, P., 2021 — source needed

Generative AI

AI that creates new content — text, images, code, audio — by learning patterns from enormous amounts of existing human-made material.

Origin: Goodfellow, I., Pouget-Abadie, J., Mirza, M., Xu, B., Warde-Farley, D., Ozair, S., Courville, A., & Bengio, Y., 2014

Current: Feuerriegel, S., Hartmann, J., Janiesch, C., & Zschech, P., 2024

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

A hypothetical AI matching capable humans at most mental tasks — not just one specialty. Doesn't exist yet; experts disagree on when, or whether.

Origin: Gubrud, M. A., 1997

Current: Morris, M., et al., 2024 — source needed

Artificial Superintelligence (ASI)

A hypothetical AI far smarter than the best humans at virtually everything — science, strategy, creativity, and social skill. Purely theoretical today.

Origin: Bostrom, N., 1998; Good, I. J., 1965

Current: Bostrom, N., 2014 — source needed

Technological Singularity

A predicted point where AI improves itself faster than humans can follow, changing society unpredictably. One famous forecast says 2045; many experts doubt it.

Origin: Ulam, S., 1958

Current: Shanahan, M., 2015 — source needed; Kurzweil, R., 2005 — source needed; Mitchell, M., 2019 — source needed

Terms About AI and Jobs

Automation (of work)

When technology performs a task instead of a person. Current research finds that automated-task jobs are losing entry-level positions.

Origin: Diebold, J., 1952

Current: Acemoglu, D., & Restrepo, P., 2019; Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., & Chen, R., 2025

See current automation data

Augmentation

When technology helps a person do their job better or faster, rather than replacing them. Augmented jobs have stayed stable or grown.

Origin: Engelbart, D. C., 1962

Current: Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., & Chen, R., 2025

See what protects augmented jobs

AI Exposure

How many of a job's tasks could AI potentially do? High exposure means possible change — not guaranteed job loss.

Origin: Frey, C. B., & Osborne, M. A., 2017; Felten, E., Raj, M., & Seamans, R., 2021

Current: Eloundou, T., Manning, S., Mishkin, P., & Rock, D., 2023

See which jobs and majors are most exposed

Job Displacement

Losing a job because technology now performs the work. Displacement is what actually happens; exposure is only what's theoretically possible.

Origin: Jacobson, L. S., LaLonde, R. J., & Sullivan, D. G., 1993

Current: U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics — source needed

See current displacement data

Policy Terms

Universal Basic Income (UBI)

Regular, unconditional cash payments from the government to everyone, regardless of employment. Proposed as a safety net if AI eliminates many jobs.

Origin: Paine, T., 1797

Current: Van Parijs, P., & Vanderborght, Y., 2017 — source needed; Vivalt, E., et al., 2024 — source needed

See the policy debate over UBI

Sovereign Wealth Fund

A government-owned investment fund whose profits benefit the public, like Alaska's fund, which pays residents yearly dividends from oil wealth.

Origin: Rozanov, A., 2005

Current: International Working Group of Sovereign Wealth Funds, 2008; Sanders, B., 2026 — source needed

See the policy debate over sovereign wealth funds
