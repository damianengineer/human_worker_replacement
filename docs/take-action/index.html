🛟 Call to Action: If Your Major Is at High Risk, Build a Plan B

  • Check your major against the exposure data first. Use the Anthropic/OpenAI occupation tables and the Stark State tier list we built earlier — if your program falls in Tier 1 (highest-confidence exposure match), don't panic, but do take the next steps seriously.

    See the exposure data and Stark State tier list

  • A minor doesn't have to mean starting over. The goal is a credential that pairs with what you're already studying, not a second full degree. A Computer Programming major, for example, pairs naturally with a Cybersecurity or Health Information Management credential — both draw on overlapping skills but sit in a different exposure tier.

  • Favor credentials with licensure or hands-on requirements. Programs like Dental Hygiene, HVACR, Electrical Maintenance, or Physical/Occupational Therapy Assistant carry the same protective factors (physical necessity, licensing requirements) that showed up throughout our research as durable barriers to automation.

    See why these barriers matter

  • Stack, don't replace. Many community college certificates (like Medical Coding, Bookkeeping, or a CDL) can be added on top of your current major in one or two semesters — a much lower-cost hedge than switching majors outright.

  • Talk to your academic advisor about a "minor" or "certificate" pathway specifically, not just a second major — advisors can often identify stackable credentials that share credits with your current coursework, cutting the time and cost of a Plan B significantly.

  • Reframe the goal: don't abandon your interest, adapt your specialization within it. If you love business but Administrative Office Professional work is exposed, consider whether a pivot toward Business Management or Marketing & Sales Management (categories with more mixed, demand-driven outlooks) keeps your interest intact while reducing risk.

  • Revisit this plan every year or two. The exposure data itself is a moving target — a field considered safe today could look different in two years, and vice versa. Build in a habit of checking back rather than treating any plan as final.

  • Don't fear it — use it as a force multiplier. The goal of every resource on this list isn't to turn you into a programmer. It's to make you someone who directs AI tools confidently, instead of someone AI tools replace.

In-Person Events

  • 🚀 Advanced Mode: Cerebral Valley — once you're ready to go further, Cerebral Valley is one of the most respected AI communities in the world, backed by Anthropic, Meta, and top venture firms, running hackathons with real prize money and direct access to people building frontier AI. It's aimed at experienced builders and founders, so it's best treated as a level-up goal, not a starting point — but it's genuinely one of the highest-value resources on this list once you're ready for it.

    Cerebral Valley, n.d.

Self-Paced Online Learning

  • Start with free, structured courses — Andrew Ng's beginner courses (via Coursera/DeepLearning.AI) are a trusted, low-cost way to actually understand how AI works, not just how to use it.

    Ng, A. / DeepLearning.AI, n.d. ; Machine Learning Specialization (Coursera)

  • Fast.ai (Jeremy Howard) takes the opposite approach from DeepLearning.AI: instead of building up theory first, it gets you training real, state-of-the-art models in the first lesson, introducing the underlying math progressively as you actually need it. Worth trying if a bottom-up course isn't clicking for you.

    Howard, J. / fast.ai, n.d.

  • Learn to build without writing code. Tools like Zapier let you connect apps and plug in AI models like Claude or ChatGPT with zero programming — a great first project. Make.com is a good next step once you outgrow Zapier's simplicity, and n8n is worth exploring specifically if you're in a computer science or programming program.

    Zapier, n.d.; Make, n.d.; n8n, n.d.

The AI Engineer World's Fair is the largest technical AI conference in the world, and every major talk is posted free on the AI Engineer YouTube channel — a way to hear directly from people building at OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind without paying a cent.

    Stanford Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence, n.d.

  • The NVIDIA Technical Blog is the closest thing to a primary source on GPU infrastructure and large-scale model deployment — useful once you want to understand the hardware and infrastructure side of AI, not just the models themselves.

  • The Batch, Andrew Ng's weekly newsletter from DeepLearning.AI, is a reliably hype-free synthesis of where AI research actually intersects with real engineering and business use — including a personal letter from Ng in every issue.

  • If you want to go straight to the research, arXiv Sanity Lite (built by Andrej Karpathy) filters and ranks the flood of new AI papers on arXiv by community signal, so you're not searching blind.

    Karpathy, A., n.d.

  • Understand what MCP is. The Model Context Protocol is how AI assistants like Claude connect to real tools and data — worth a basic understanding since it's becoming the standard way AI gets "hands" to do real work.

    Anthropic, n.d.

  • Learn the privacy basics before you rely on AI daily. Understand what happens to information you type into a chatbot, and check whether your school or future employer has AI usage policies — treat this the same way you'd treat any other digital privacy habit.

Online Communities

  • Join a beginner-friendly community. Learn AI Together (a Discord community run by Towards AI) is built specifically for people learning AI from scratch — a good first stop before anything more advanced.

⚠️ AI Privacy and Security

AI tools don't just add new privacy and security risks — they often make your existing ones bigger. Every habit you already should have around passwords, personal information, and sensitive data applies to AI too, and then some: what you type into a chatbot can be logged, stored, used to improve a company's models, or exposed if that company gets breached.

Be especially careful before you paste personal information, passwords, financial details, health information, or confidential work material into an AI tool — or connect an AI tool to a system that handles money, medical records, or other sensitive functions. If you wouldn't hand it to a stranger, don't hand it to an AI tool without knowing exactly where that information goes.

  • Learn how AI systems actually get attacked, hands-on. Prompt injection — tricking an AI into ignoring its instructions or revealing things it shouldn't — is one of the most common ways AI tools get exploited. This curated list of free, hands-on practice challenges (including Gandalf, Prompt Airlines, and PortSwigger's LLM Attacks Lab) lets you see it happen yourself, safely.

    damianengineer, n.d.

  • Know the frameworks security researchers actually use. MITRE ATLAS catalogs real-world attacks against AI systems, and the OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications lists the most common ways AI tools get exploited in practice — both are free, and both are built for exactly this kind of self-education.

    MITRE, n.d.; OWASP, 2025

  • Know the legal line, not just the technical one. The EU AI Act bans some AI uses outright, including AI that manipulates people in ways that cause real harm and mass, untargeted scraping of faces to build facial recognition databases. Even outside the EU, it's a useful signal for what regulators consider not just risky, but flatly unacceptable.

    European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2024 ; Plain-language summary (EU AI Act Service Desk)

🏛️ Call to Action: Be an Informed Citizen on AI Policy

First, understand the debate

Before tracking any bill, it helps to understand the two big proposals for sharing AI's gains — and the honest case against each. These aren't fringe ideas or settled plans: they're insurance policies being debated now for a future nobody can yet measure.

Universal Basic Income (UBI)

Regular, unconditional cash payments to everyone, employed or not — an income floor under every person.

The case for: If AI displaces workers faster than new jobs appear, systems tied to having a job — wages, employer benefits, payroll-tax-funded programs like Social Security — start to fail people through no fault of their own. A guaranteed floor catches everyone, automatically, without deciding who "deserves" help. The idea is older than the microchip: Thomas Paine proposed a version in 1797, and modern trials have tested it in the real world.

Paine, T., 1797; Van Parijs, P., & Vanderborght, Y., 2017; Vivalt, E., Rhodes, E., Bartik, A. W., Broockman, D. E., Krause, P., & Miller, S., 2024

The case against: It's expensive at national scale, and the trial evidence is genuinely mixed on how it changes work and wellbeing. And the strongest objection comes from our own Evidence page: skeptical economists argue the data doesn't yet show displacement at anywhere near the scale that would make UBI necessary — and history shows new kinds of jobs tend to appear when old ones change.

Kolko, J., 2026; Autor, D., Chin, C., Salomons, A., & Seegmiller, B., 2024

Sovereign Wealth Fund

A government-owned investment fund whose profits pay dividends to the public — shared ownership of the gains, not just a payment.

The case for: If AI concentrates enormous profits in the companies that own it, a public fund gives everyone a stake in those gains instead of a check funded by taxes. It's not hypothetical: Alaska has paid residents a yearly dividend from oil wealth for decades, and dozens of countries run such funds under agreed international principles. That's the model behind the sovereign-wealth-fund bills now in Congress.

Rozanov, A., 2005; International Working Group of Sovereign Wealth Funds, 2008; Sanders, B., 2026

The case against: Where does the fund's money come from — and does the government picking investments create its own problems? Alaska's fund taps a physical resource the state already owned; AI profits belong to private companies. And the same skeptical evidence applies: if displacement stays modest, a massive new fund solves a problem we may not have.

Kolko, J., 2026

This site doesn't endorse either proposal. Our argument is simpler: these debates are happening now, while you're in school — and you will live with the outcome longer than almost anyone currently voting on it. Whatever you conclude, conclude it from the evidence. That's what the steps below are for.

  • Track real bills yourself — don't rely on headlines. Congress.gov is the official, nonpartisan source for bill text and status. As of this research, Senator Bernie Sanders' American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act (S.4825) has been introduced and referred to the Senate Finance Committee, with zero cosponsors so far — a good example of how far a proposal is from becoming law, versus how it's often covered in the news.

    Sanders, B., 2026

  • Know there's more than one proposal on the table. A related bill, the American Sovereign Wealth Fund Exploration Act (H.R. 3116), has separately been introduced in the House by Rep. Morgan McGarvey — worth knowing that this isn't a single-party or single-bill issue.

    McGarvey, M., 2025

  • Understand what's actually being proposed before forming an opinion. Read the plain differences between a Universal Basic Income (regular cash payments to everyone) and a Sovereign Wealth Fund (government-owned investment fund that pays dividends, like Alaska's oil fund) — these solve different problems and have different trade-offs, which we covered earlier in this project.

    Universal Basic Income (glossary); Sovereign Wealth Fund (glossary)

  • Connect the policy debate to something concrete: Social Security. The Social Security Trust Fund is projected to run short of money around 2034, according to the latest official Trustees Report — a real, non-speculative reason this issue affects your own retirement, not just an abstract future.

    Social Security Administration, Office of the Chief Actuary, 2026

  • Read multiple sources before deciding what you think. Go back to the evenhanded evidence we gathered earlier — the strongest research (like the Stanford "Canaries" study) shows real but limited effects so far, while skeptical, credible economists (like the Brookings review) argue it's too early to know for sure. Good citizenship on this topic means holding both of those facts at once, not picking the version that confirms what you already believed.

    Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., & Chen, R., 2025; Kolko, J., 2026 ; Revisit the full evidence and counterarguments

  • Show up to where decisions actually get made. Look for local town halls, university panels, or state legislative hearings on AI and labor policy — these are often less partisan and more substantive than national media coverage.

  • Vote with the long-term stakes in mind, not just today's headlines. Whatever you think about specific proposals like a sovereign wealth fund or UBI, the underlying question — how should the gains from AI be shared, and how do we fund retirement if fewer people are working traditional jobs — will still matter for your entire working life, long after any single election cycle.
