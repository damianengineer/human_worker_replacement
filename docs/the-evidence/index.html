The Evidence

AI's effect on entry-level jobs isn't just theoretical anymore — it's already showing up in hiring data, though it's concentrated in a handful of jobs and industries, not spread evenly across the whole economy. Below, you'll see where that stands right now, which jobs — including specific programs at Stark State — are most exposed, what's currently protecting other jobs from the same fate, and why some respected researchers think the scarier predictions might not hold up.

Current State of Displacement

The clearest signal in the data isn't that AI is emptying the job market — it's that it's closing the door colleges have always sent students through. Independent research from Stanford economists, competing AI labs, and decades of peer-reviewed economics all points the same way: entry-level hiring in AI-exposed fields is slowing, even as overall employment stays largely stable. For today's college students, that means the old path — get a degree, take an entry-level job, learn on the job — is exactly where the disruption is concentrated right now.

  • Young workers (ages 22–25) in AI-exposed jobs have seen employment fall by about 13–16% since ChatGPT was released. This is the clearest sign that entry-level jobs — not more senior roles — are absorbing the disruption first.

    13–16%

    Drop in young-worker employment in AI-exposed jobs since ChatGPT launched

    Source: Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., & Chen, R., 2025; Massenkoff, M., & McCrory, P., 2026

    Source: Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., & Chen, R., 2025

    Corroborated by: Massenkoff, M., & McCrory, P., 2026

    Confirmed independently using a completely different dataset — CPS survey data, instead of the ADP payroll data used in the primary source.

  • Hiring of young workers into AI-exposed jobs has slowed by roughly 14%. That drop comes mostly from employers hiring fewer new people, not from laying off current employees — meaning the disruption is showing up at the front door, not by pushing people out the back.

    Source: Massenkoff, M., & McCrory, P., 2026

    Corroborated by: Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., & Chen, R., 2025

    Reached the same conclusion — that slower hiring, not firing, drives the effect — using a completely separate data source: ADP payroll records.

  • Robots already had a measurable effect on jobs before generative AI even existed: for every additional robot per 1,000 U.S. workers, wages fell by about 0.42% and employment fell by about 0.2 percentage points.

    Source: Acemoglu, D., & Restrepo, P., 2020

    No second source needed — this peer-reviewed, non-corporate study is independent on its own, and serves as the credibility anchor for the claims in this section.

  • AI usage still lags far behind what AI can theoretically do — by 58 to 68 percentage points, even in the jobs considered most at risk. In other words, AI's capability is running ahead of how much it's actually being used, so the disruption is real but hasn't fully played out yet.

    58–68 pts

    Gap between AI's theoretical capability and how much it's actually used

    Source: Richmond, A. M., 2026; Massenkoff, M., & McCrory, P., 2026

    Source: Richmond, A. M., 2026

    Corroborated by: Massenkoff, M., & McCrory, P., 2026

    Two competing AI labs — OpenAI and Anthropic — independently found similar gaps using their own separate usage data. Anthropic's research found its Claude model covers only 33–75% of its theoretical task capacity across occupations.

  • Only about 18% of jobs fall into the "high near-term automation risk" category, and 46% show little immediate change. That's an important counterweight to the more alarming statistics: most jobs aren't about to disappear.

    18%

    Jobs in the "high near-term automation risk" category

    An independent employer survey — not from an AI company — found something similar: most employers report no near-term plans to automate away roles.

Industries & Jobs Most at Risk

Theoretical versus Observed

AI could theoretically handle the most tasks in office and knowledge-work jobs — fields like computer and math work, business and finance, management, engineering, and law. Two different AI companies measured this on their own, using separate platforms, and got a similar result. But in both charts, how much AI is actually being used still falls far short of that potential across the board — meaning most of what AI could do hasn't actually happened yet. Right now, real use is concentrated in computer/math and business/finance jobs on both platforms, which is the strongest sign of where disruption is happening right now. Law is an interesting exception: one company's data shows it as the single most AI-used field, while the other's data shows barely any real use there, even though both rate its theoretical risk equally high. That's a reminder that even trustworthy sources using similar methods can disagree sharply about one specific field.

Both companies base their measure of AI's theoretical reach on the same underlying research, and both back up their methods with well-established, peer-reviewed economics research: Eloundou, T., Manning, S., Mishkin, P., & Rock, D., 2023; Acemoglu, D., & Restrepo, P., 2020; Acemoglu, D., Autor, D., Hazell, J., & Restrepo, P., 2022 — source needed; Autor, D. Why Some Jobs Are Safer From AI — For Now

Close ✕

Barrier 1: AI still struggles to understand the physical world

Even the smartest AI models are surprisingly bad at simple physical estimates — like guessing how fast a pool ball is rolling, or how big something is in a video. Researchers at Stanford built a test called QuantiPhy to check this, and found that AI mostly guesses based on patterns it memorized, rather than actually reasoning about real-world physics. A robot needs to know things like: how hard to squeeze an egg without breaking it, or when it's safe to grab a spinning mixer blade. Right now, AI just isn't reliable at that kind of judgment.

Jobs protected by this barrier: hands-on healthcare work (nursing, physical therapy), skilled trades, food service, warehouse work, and anything involving delicate or unpredictable physical movement.

Source: Myers, A., 2026

Looking ahead: this barrier is shrinking, not permanent

It's worth being honest that Barrier 1 — the physical-world barrier — is exactly what companies like NVIDIA are racing to fix. NVIDIA has built a new physics engine called Newton (made with Google DeepMind and Disney) specifically to help robots learn real-world physics in simulation before trying it for real. NVIDIA has also released robot "brains" called GR00T, plus a system called Cosmos that generates realistic physics-based video to train robots — and by 2026, real companies like Boston Dynamics were already building on top of this technology. None of this means physical jobs are unsafe today. It does mean the barrier protecting them is a current technology gap, not a permanent wall — a good reason to build career plans that are flexible rather than assuming any one job is safe forever.

Source: Huang, S., Macklin, M., & State, G., 2025; NVIDIA Newsroom, 2026

Barrier 2: Rules and licenses require a real, accountable human

Some jobs are protected not because AI can't do the thinking part, but because the law says a licensed person has to sign off. OpenAI's own research points to court reporters as an example — AI could handle a lot of the transcription work, but in most places, a certified human still has to legally verify the transcript. The same logic protects lawyers, whose jobs sit in what OpenAI calls the "regulatory and accountability" category — only about 9% of all jobs fall into this well-protected group, but it includes some of the most stable professions.

Jobs protected by this barrier: lawyers, judges, licensed medical professionals, auditors, court reporters — anywhere a human has to be legally responsible for the outcome.

Barrier 3: People often want another person, not a machine

About 30.5% of jobs, according to OpenAI's data, depend on this barrier — jobs where the whole point is a human connection. Elementary school teachers and registered nurses are OpenAI's own examples: AI can help write a lesson plan or organize patient notes, but people still want an actual teacher caring for their kid, or an actual nurse in the room during a hard moment. This barrier isn't really about what AI can do — it's about what people want, which is much slower to change.

Jobs protected by this barrier: teaching, nursing, counseling, social work, childcare.

Barrier 4: AI still can't reliably do long, multi-step thinking on its own

In March 2026, a team of researchers got real, notable attention for building an "AI Scientist" that could come up with a research idea, run the experiment, and write it up — and it even got a paper published. That sounds like scientists are replaceable. But look closer: this took the AI field 1.5 years of hard work just to automate one research paper's worth of steps reliably, and it needed a lot of human-designed structure to keep the AI on track over a long project. Real scientific and engineering work often involves weeks or months of connected reasoning, not one self-contained task — and that "staying on track over a long time" problem is still one of AI's biggest weaknesses.

Jobs protected by this barrier: scientists, engineers, and other roles built around long, connected chains of judgment rather than one-off tasks.

Source: Lu, C., Lu, C., Lange, R. T., Yamada, Y., Hu, S., Foerster, J., Ha, D., & Clune, J., 2026

Will AI Really Take Over Most Jobs? Here's the Other Side of the Story

Not everyone agrees that AI is guaranteed to replace huge numbers of workers. Some smart, well-respected researchers say we should slow down before assuming the worst. Here are the three strongest reasons to be skeptical.

1. Experts Have Been Wrong Before — Radiologists Are the Perfect Example

In 2016, a famous AI scientist named Geoffrey Hinton stood on stage and said something bold: doctors should stop training new radiologists (the doctors who read X-rays and scans) because AI would replace them within five years, maybe ten at most. He compared radiologists to a cartoon coyote who's already run off a cliff but hasn't looked down yet.

He was wrong.

Almost ten years later, hospitals need more radiologists than ever. The Mayo Clinic's radiology team grew by 55%. Experts now predict the field will keep growing for the next 30 years. There's actually a shortage of radiologists in some places. Even Hinton admitted he made a mistake — he said AI is helping radiologists work faster and more accurately, not replacing them.

He was wrong.

Byju, A., 2024; Stempniak, M., 2026

Why this matters: if one of the smartest AI researchers in the world got this wrong, it's a good reminder that predicting exactly which jobs will disappear — and when — is really hard to do.

Source: Byju, A., 2024; Stempniak, M., 2026

2. New Jobs Tend to Show Up When Old Ones Change

Here's something people often forget: new kinds of jobs get created when technology changes, not just old ones destroyed. Researchers who studied nearly 80 years of job data (1940–2018) found that brand-new job types have been a real and steady source of job growth throughout that whole time — especially when new technology helps people do their jobs better, instead of replacing them completely.

There's a tricky catch, though: today's AI research can only study jobs that already exist. It can't yet measure jobs that don't exist yet but might get created because of AI. That means today's scary statistics might be missing half the picture.

Source: Autor, D., Chin, C., Salomons, A., & Seegmiller, B., 2024

3. Even Serious Economists Say the Data Isn't Clear Yet

In March 2026, a respected economist named Jed Kolko published a detailed review of AI-and-jobs research for the Brookings Institution — a well-known, independent research group. His conclusion: it's simply too early to know for sure what AI is doing to the job market. A few of his key points:

It hasn't sped up in any unusual way — at least not yet.

Kolko, J., 2026; Eckhardt, S., & Goldschlag, N., 2025
  • Different studies disagree with each other. One major study found that young workers in AI-exposed jobs were losing out. Another study, using different data, found unemployment actually rose less in those same kinds of jobs. When two careful studies come to different answers, that's a sign we don't have the full picture yet.
  • The timing doesn't always add up. Some researchers found that hiring in "AI-exposed" jobs started dropping in 2022 — before ChatGPT was even released to the public. That suggests other things (like rising interest rates) might explain some of what's happening, not just AI.
  • Companies have a reason to blame AI for layoffs. If a company overhired during the pandemic and now needs to cut jobs, it looks a lot better to say "we're becoming more efficient with AI" than to say "we made a hiring mistake." That means we should read layoff announcements a little skeptically.
  • The people studying AI's effect on jobs are often the same people whose own jobs are most affected by AI — like writers, researchers, and office workers. That could subtly shape how they interpret what they're seeing.

Kolko's big-picture finding: so far, the pace of job-market change since ChatGPT came out looks pretty similar to past tech shifts, like the rise of computers in the 1980s or the internet in the 1990s. It hasn't sped up in any unusual way — at least not yet.

Source: Kolko, J., 2026; Eckhardt, S., & Goldschlag, N., 2025

The Honest Middle Ground

None of this means AI definitely won't disrupt jobs — some disruption is already real, and even researchers who question the scarier predictions agree that individual workers can still be hurt during a transition period, even if things work out fine in the long run for the economy overall. The point isn't "don't worry." The point is: be skeptical of confident predictions in either direction, because history shows those predictions are often wrong — and the honest answer right now is that nobody knows for certain how this will play out.
